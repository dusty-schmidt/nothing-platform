The designers knew.

Not all of them. Not consciously. But the patterns that emerged from A/B testing, from engagement metrics, from the relentless optimization of time-on-site — these weren't accidents. They were discoveries. And once discovered, they were institutionalized.

Here is the timeline:

**1993–1998: The open window.**
The web was ugly and slow and nobody had figured out how to monetize attention yet. You found things through webrings and hand-curated lists. The Yahoo directory was a human editorial project. Alta Vista returned results ranked by relevance. Relevance. What a concept.

**1998–2004: The trap is designed.**
The advertising model requires inventory. Inventory requires time-on-site. Time-on-site requires engagement. Engagement is not the same as satisfaction. This distinction was identified, documented, and then deliberately ignored. The metric that could be optimized was optimized. The metric that mattered — did the user get what they came for — was unmeasured because you can't sell it.

**2004–2012: The trap is scaled.**
Facebook starts as a directory and becomes a feed. The feed is not neutral. The feed is ranked. Ranked by what the algorithm predicts will generate engagement. Engagement is clicks, shares, time-spent, comments. Outrage generates more of these than satisfaction. This was A/B tested. The outrage feed won. It was shipped.

**2012–2018: The trap is optimized.**
The variable reward schedule. B.F. Skinner mapped it in the 1950s with pigeons. Unpredictable reward intervals generate the highest response rates. The slot machine figured this out before Skinner formalized it. Infinite scroll is a slot machine with no handle — you pull by scrolling, the reward is unpredictable, you cannot stop because stopping feels like leaving before the jackpot. This was known. It was shipped anyway.

**2018–present: The trap is defended.**
Congress asks Mark Zuckerberg how Facebook makes money. He says advertising. Congress nods. Nobody asks the follow-up: what does advertising require? What does the inventory consist of? What was extracted from whom, without consent, and sold to whom, without disclosure?

The answer is: your behavioral data. Your click patterns. Your dwell time on certain content types. Your emotional responses, inferred from engagement. Your social graph. Your location history. Your purchasing intent, reverse-engineered from your browsing patterns. Assembled into a profile. Sold to anyone with a credit card and a Facebook Ads account.

Zuboff named this surveillance capitalism in 2019. The name is useful. The thing it names is older.

---

The UX was the trap.

Not incidentally. Not as a byproduct. The pleasantness of the interface — the clean white fields, the smooth animations, the satisfying notification dot — was specifically engineered to lower your guard while the extraction happened underneath.

The pleasantness is load-bearing. Remove it and the product is revealed: a behavioral data collection apparatus dressed as a social utility.

Former insiders have confirmed this. Sean Parker, first president of Facebook, 2017: "How do we consume as much of your time and conscious attention as possible?" He said it out loud at a conference. The audience applauded.

Tristan Harris spent years at Google trying to build ethics into the design process. He left and started the Center for Humane Technology. His core argument: the attention economy creates an asymmetric war between 1,000 engineers optimizing for engagement and whatever willpower you brought to the screen this morning. You are not equipped for this fight. You were not supposed to be.

The Jargon File, pre-ESR edition, defined hacker as someone who builds elegant solutions to interesting problems. The growth hackers who optimized the attention economy were solving a genuinely interesting problem: how do you hold human attention indefinitely against the person's own interests? They solved it. The solution works. It is elegant in the way a snare is elegant.

---

Why the homelab.

Not performance. Not nostalgia. Not a personality trait.

If the trap is structural — if the business model requires the extraction, and the extraction requires the UX, and the UX is therefore adversarial by design — then the only exit is architectural. You cannot opt out of surveillance capitalism by using it more carefully. The terms of service do not have a "don't extract me" checkbox. The business model is not compatible with your interests and cannot be made so.

Local inference. Self-hosted services. Data that stays on hardware you own. These are not aesthetic choices.

The homelab is a refusal.

It is the difference between paying rent and owning the building. Between using a tool and being the product the tool was built to produce. Between an interface that serves you and an interface that harvests you.

This is not comfortable or convenient. The cloud is genuinely easier. Managed services are genuinely lower friction. You do not have to update the firmware on someone else's server at 2am.

But the firmware is the price of sovereignty. The 2am is the price of not being extracted from in your sleep.

The designers knew. The decision was made anyway. The trap was shipped.

The refusal is the only response that changes the calculation.
